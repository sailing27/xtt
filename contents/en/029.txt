The rain on the windowpane smeared the neon sign across the street into a bleeding wound of pink light. Another night, another client with a story thinner than the cheap bourbon in my desk drawer. She sat across from me, twisting a lace handkerchief into a desperate little rope.

“He’s vanished, Mr. Vale. Like smoke.”

They always do. People in this city are half ghost already. I could smell her perfume—jasmine and regret—over the scent of damp wool and old lies.

“Smoke leaves a stain,” I said, my voice a gravel road. “Everyone leaves a stain. The question is what they were burning.”

She placed a photograph on the desk. A man’s face, already blurring at the edges in my memory. Another soul dissolving into the perpetual, dreamlike drizzle. My job was to find the stain before the rain washed it all away.
