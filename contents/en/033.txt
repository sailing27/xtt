The sunsets here are the most beautiful I’ve ever seen. Every evening, the sky bleeds into shades of honey and rose, just like the picture in my old storybook. I tell my little sister, Elara, about the real sunsets I remember—the ones from Before.

“They were even warmer,” I whisper, tucking the frayed blanket around her. “You could feel the light on your skin, like a kiss.”

She smiles, her eyes bright. “I can feel it now.”

I nod, swallowing the truth. The window is just a high-resolution screen; the “sunset” a scheduled program. The real warmth left with the real sun, blocked by the permanent gray haze. But my memory is a fragile, precious thing. If I let it fade, what will we have left? So I describe the kiss of sunlight, and in her believing smile, I almost feel it too.
