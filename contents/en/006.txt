The thing about my dad’s “business trips” is the glitter. It gets everywhere. In the carpet, under my fingernails, a persistent shimmer on the cuffs of his good suit.

“Just a little side hustle, champ,” he’d say, winking as he packed his briefcase full of what looked like oversized, rainbow-dusted sugar cubes. “Brightening people’s lives.”

I believed him. I was ten. The men who came to the door at odd hours, their eyes too wide, their laughter too sharp, were just enthusiastic clients. Mom’s tight silence was worry over travel logistics.

Then Officer Jenkins showed up, not with a warrant, but with a confiscated piñata from the school fair. It had burst, coating the evidence room in a tell-tale, iridescent haze. He held up a single, sparkling fragment. “Recognize this brand of cheer, Mikey?”

The floor felt very still under my feet. All I could see was the glitter, clinging to everything, revealing everything. My dad didn’t sell joy. He just sold the desperate, crumbling shine of it.
