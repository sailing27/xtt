“You can’t keep it.”

“It’s just a box, Leo.”

“Open it. I dare you.”

My fingers traced the cold, lacquered wood. The hinges were silent. Inside, nestled on velvet the color of a fading bruise, was a single, tarnished key.

“Where did you get this?”

“The old oak. Where the roots split the sidewalk.”

“That’s not an answer.” His whisper was frayed wire. “Put it back.”

“Why?”

“Because some doors shouldn’t be unlocked.” He took a step forward, the floorboard groaning under his weight. The air tasted metallic, like a storm pressing down. “It doesn’t belong to you.”

I closed the lid. The click was final. But the key’s shape was already seared behind my eyes, a phantom weight in my palm. I knew I’d find the door.
