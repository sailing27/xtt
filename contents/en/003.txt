The air in the royal portrait gallery was thick with dust and disappointment. Prince Alistair, aged twelve and three-quarters, slumped on a velvet stool, his chin propped in his hand. The court painter, a man named Finchley with perpetually paint-stained cuffs, dabbed furiously at the canvas.

“A slight smile, Your Highness,” Finchley pleaded. “Think of… jousting. Or plum pudding.”

Alistair thought of his tutor’s endless lectures on taxation. His expression grew more dour.

“Perhaps a noble scowl, then? A look of fierce resolve?”

The prince thought of the frog he’d accidentally stepped on in the garden that morning. A genuine grimace of remorse twisted his features.

“Perfect!” cried Finchley, capturing the look of profound amphibian guilt for posterity. Alistair sighed. Future historians would have a lot of questions.
