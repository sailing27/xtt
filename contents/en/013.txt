The city slept beneath its glass dome. I pressed my palm to the cold, curved wall.

“They say the stars are dead,” I whispered.

“They say a lot of things,” Leo answered, his voice a soft echo in the empty observatory. His fingers traced the ghost of a constellation on the glass. “My grandmother remembered them. She called them ‘the old fires.’”

“Do you believe her?”

“I believe the ache in my chest when I look up.” He turned from the sterile, artificial twilight. “It’s not for the stars we lost. It’s for the ones we’ll never see.”

Outside, the sunlamps began their slow fade to simulated night. A single, impossible point of silver light winked beyond the dome. We held our breath. It was gone.

“A flaw in the glass,” Leo said, but his hand found mine. We didn’t let go.
