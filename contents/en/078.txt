The water ration is half what it was yesterday. I watch my sister’s hands tremble as she divides the last bottle. She says it’s fair, but I saw her pour a little extra into my cup when she thought I wasn’t looking. The sun bleaches everything to a dull white, even the truth.

“Drink,” she says, her voice cracking like the dry earth. “You need it more.”

I take the cup. The water tastes like metal and guilt. I know she hasn’t had a sip since dawn. I pretend to drink, letting most of it trickle back into the bottle when she turns to scan the empty horizon. She’ll never know. Some lies are necessary. Some are the only kindness left in a world that’s forgotten how to be kind.
