The memory of my sister’s laugh is the clearest thing I have. It’s a perfect, crystalline sound in my mind, untouched by the passage of years. The doctors say it’s a sign of the neural degradation, that my brain is clinging to one stable point as the rest unravels. They tell me she died in the evacuation shuttle crash a decade ago.

But I see her. Every morning, she’s there by my window, her outline shimmering slightly in the dawn light. “You’re getting better, Leo,” she whispers, her voice a ghost of a sound only I can hear.

I know she isn’t real. The medical scans prove it. Yet when she smiles, the hollow ache in my chest eases, and for a moment, the world doesn’t feel so empty and cold.
