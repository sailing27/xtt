The ghost in my attic wasn't a wailing specter, but a chronic complainer. I found him perched on a dusty trunk, glaring at a cobweb.

“The acoustics up here are atrocious,” he grumbled, his form flickering like a bad bulb. “And the draft? Unconscionable. My ectoplasm is practically goosefleshed.”

I blinked. “Most ghosts moan about unfinished business.”

“My business,” he sniffed, “is critiquing the substandard haunting conditions of this domicile. The previous resident, a Victorian seamstress, had impeccable atmospheric control.” He gestured a translucent hand toward a cracked window. “This is sloppy. I’m writing a strongly worded missive to the Spectral Housing Authority.”

I offered him a blanket. He looked at it with profound disdain. “Wool? In this humidity? You’re as bad as the landlord.” I decided then that my haunting was less a horror story and more a very tedious, very dead roommate situation.
