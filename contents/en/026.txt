The last slice of pizza sat between them like a contested island. Rain blurred the diner window, turning the neon sign across the street into a watery smear of pink.

“You have it,” Leo said, pushing the plate toward Mia. His voice was too casual.

“No, you,” she insisted, not meeting his eyes. “You always finish the crusts.”

A silence stretched, filled by the hiss of the grill and the low hum of a forgotten pop song. This wasn’t about pizza. This was about her moving away tomorrow, about the thousand after-school afternoons that were ending right here in this sticky vinyl booth.

Finally, Leo picked up the slice. He took a small, solemn bite, then handed the rest to her. “Shared custody,” he mumbled around the cheese.

She took it, her smile wobbling. They finished it together, not tasting the pepperoni, only the sharp, sweet ache of goodbye.
