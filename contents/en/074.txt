The ‘67 Impala’s air conditioning had died somewhere in Nebraska, leaving us marinating in our own regret. My brother, Leo, was navigating by a wrinkled gas station map and sheer spite.

“Next exit has the world’s second-largest ball of twine,” he announced, not looking up.

“We’re not stopping.”

“It’s a landmark, Evie. A testament to human dedication.”

“It’s twine.” I watched a dead cornfield blur past. “And we’re running from our parents’ divorce, not toward folk art.”

He was quiet for a mile. The vinyl seat stuck to my skin. “Maybe,” he finally said, his voice uncharacteristically soft, “the dedication is the point.”

I glanced over. His jaw was set, but his eyes were red. He wasn’t talking about the twine. The car hummed, a metal box holding our shattered world together, one dusty mile at a time.
