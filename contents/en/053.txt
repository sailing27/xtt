The city breathed in slow, mechanical sighs. Above the smog, the sky was a perpetual bruise of purple and orange, lit by the ghostly advertisements that flickered across the glass towers. Below, the streets were silent, swept clean by drones that hummed like drowsy bees.

Lena pressed her palm against the cool window of her pod, her breath fogging the reinforced glass. She could just make out the silhouette of the old library, its stone arches crumbling under a blanket of luminous moss. It was forbidden, of course. That’s why she went.

“The air tastes different there,” she’d told her brother, her voice a whisper in their sterile room. “It tastes like stories.”

He’d only blinked, his eyes reflecting the data-stream on the wall. But Lena remembered the taste of paper and dust, a secret sweetness on her tongue, promising something the city had long forgotten.
