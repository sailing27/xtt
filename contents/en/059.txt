The summer I turned twelve, the old lighthouse on Harper’s Point was still lit. I’d watch its beam carve a path across the bay from my bedroom window, a silent, sweeping companion to my insomnia. That was the year I found the first bottle. It washed up at dawn, seaweed clinging to its green glass, a cork wedged tight. Inside, no message, just a single, perfect sand dollar. I showed it to my grandpa over pancakes.

“Haven’t seen one of those in years,” he murmured, his knobby finger tracing its fragile ridges. His eyes grew distant, fixed on the lighthouse. “Your grandmother used to collect them.”

A week later, a second bottle arrived. This one held a tarnished key. The lighthouse beam seemed to pause on the water, waiting. I knew, with a certainty that warmed my bones, they were for me.
