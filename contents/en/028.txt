My cleats bite into the track, each step a frantic prayer. The baton is slick in my palm, a cylinder of pure dread. I can hear him—the slap of his spikes, the ragged pull of his breath. My hand flies back, fingers splayed, a target waiting for the blow.

“Go!”

The command is a jolt of lightning. I’m already moving, arm extended, trusting the physics of our practiced pass. The smooth plastic slaps into my waiting grip. For a heartbeat, it’s just me and the weight in my hand. Then the roar of the crowd crashes in, a wave of sound at my back. I lean into the curve, legs burning, the finish line a distant promise. The baton isn’t just wood and metal anymore. It’s my team’s hope, and I’m the last one carrying it.
