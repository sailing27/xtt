The rain on the windowpane smeared the city lights into tears of neon. She said her name was Violet, but the scent of old paper and regret that clung to her whispered otherwise. “I need you to find a memory,” she murmured, her gloved fingers tracing the rim of her empty glass. “I think I lost it on a Tuesday.”

I should have said no. Memories are slippery clients; they change shape in the telling. But the way the lamplight caught the silver in her hair, like frost on a forgotten window—I was already lost. “Memories aren’t things you find,” I said, my voice a low rumble in the quiet bar. “They’re things that find you.”

She smiled, a fragile, breaking thing. “Then let’s get lost together.” And I knew, with a detective’s cold certainty, that I was no longer solving her case, but becoming part of it.
