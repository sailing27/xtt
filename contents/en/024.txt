The rain made everything look smudged, like a bad painting. I watched it streak the window of the cop car, blurring the neon sign of the diner across the street. My knuckles were raw, but I couldn’t remember why.

“You want to tell us what happened, kid?” The officer’s voice was tired, like old leather.

I shrugged. “He fell.”

That was the truth. Marcus fell. I just didn’t mention the shove, the crack of his head against the dumpster, the way he didn’t get up. The memory felt slippery, like something I’d seen in a movie. Maybe I had. My mind did that sometimes, edited things for my own protection. The metallic taste of fear was real, though, coating my tongue. I focused on the rain, letting it wash the details away, until all that remained was the simple, clean fact: he fell. They’d believe it. They always did.
