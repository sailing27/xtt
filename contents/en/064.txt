The first time I stole something, my hands didn’t shake. I thought they would. Everyone says they do. But my fingers were steady as they slipped the can of soup from the shelf and into the deep pocket of my coat. It was the silence that got me—the thick, dusty quiet of the corner store after school, broken only by Mr. Henderson’s wheezy cough from behind the counter. I walked out, the weight of the can a cold anchor against my thigh. It wasn’t hunger that made me do it. It was the look on Mom’s face that morning, empty as our cupboard. I needed to prove the silence in our house wrong, to fill it with something, even if it was just the soft *thump* of a stolen dinner hitting the bottom of a pot.
