The rain wasn’t the clean, storybook kind. It was a greasy drizzle that made the city’s neon bleed into the cracked asphalt. Leo hunched under the awning of Mr. Chen’s bodega, the smell of wet concrete and stale fried dough thick in the air. Inside, the old man was counting change, his movements slow as a clock running down.

“You’re late,” Mr. Chen said, not looking up. His voice was a dry leaf scraping pavement.

“Bus broke down,” Leo mumbled, shoving his damp hands into threadbare pockets. The lie tasted like copper.

Mr. Chen finally met his eyes, holding out a brown paper bag, warm from the egg custard tart inside. “Your mother already called. The bus is fine.” He didn’t ask about the split lip, the shaky hands. The kindness, unspoken, was a solid thing in the damp air, heavier than any question.
